Vaccine Confidence

Connecting values, media, and culture to move audiences towards higher childhood vaccination uptake

Harmony Labs, FrameWorks Institute, AAP

Introduction

Vaccine Confidence Is Formed Long Before the Doctor's Office

Decisions about childhood vaccination are rarely made in clinical settings alone. Long before a parent talks to a pediatrician, they have absorbed years of stories about health, risk, care, and responsibility from the culture around them.

Key Insight: Vaccine confidence is not built in a single interaction. It accumulates over time, across culture.

Not Everyone Sees the Same Vaccine Story

Vaccine-related stories appear everywhere - but not in the same way for everyone. Different audiences inhabit different media ecosystems, and those ecosystems carry distinct narratives about vaccines.

Key Insight: There is no single "vaccine conversation." There are many parallel ones, happening at once.

The Same Message Can Reassure or Alienate

People carry cultural stories with them into every new interaction about vaccines. Past media exposure, alongside personal experiences with caregiving and healthcare, shapes how new information is interpreted.

Key Insight: When communication fails, it is often not because the information is wrong, but because the cultural context is mismatched.

Culture Is Where Vaccine Confidence Is Won or Lost

If vaccine confidence is formed through culture across entertainment, social media, and everyday storytelling, then information alone is not enough. A cultural strategy is required to engage the values, identities, and emotional logic that shape how different people interpret health decisions.

Key Insight: Meeting families in their cultural worlds, before positions harden, is no longer optional. It is the condition for rebuilding confidence.

FrameWorks and the American Academy of Pediatrics partnered with Harmony Labs to understand how culture, media, and values shape childhood vaccine confidence, and to identify how public health can engage families through the narratives and media they already engage with.

Key Learnings

A Confidence Bubble Shapes Vaccine Communication

The audience most aligned with public-health guidance experiences vaccination as simple, straightforward, and socially agreed upon. Their confidence is reinforced by high institutional trust and by media environments that normalize vaccination as routine care.

From inside this bubble, it can be difficult to see why other parents struggle, hesitate, or ask questions. Over time, this blind spot has quietly shaped vaccine communication strategies, anchoring them in assumptions that do not hold for other audiences.

Misreading Values Can Create Mistrust

When parents and caregivers express hesitation, those responses can be interpreted as misinformation, ignorance, or a lack of scientific literacy.

Our data shows something different. Concerns about childhood vaccination are values-driven and predictable. That means they are addressable. When communication fails to recognize the values underneath hesitation, it can erode trust and deepen distance.

Rebuilding confidence requires shifting from a 'right or wrong' lens to a values-based one, where concerns are treated as meaningful starting points rather than mistakes to be corrected.

Most People Believe Vaccines Work

The majority of Americans, across all five audiences, agree that vaccines have protected them personally.

Confidence is strongest among Trusting the Process and Caring for My People, moderate but open among Checking the Details and Protecting My Family, and cautious among Calling My Own Shots.

The overall landscape is not one of broad vaccine doubt but of different forms of confidence, each resting on its own foundation: system reliability, practical benefits, alignment with family values, transparent details, or autonomy.

Distrust in Institutions Is Universal

Even among the most pro-vaccine audiences, many people believe the healthcare system is influenced by profit. This institutional skepticism is one of the few attitudes shared across all five audiences.

Importantly, diminished trust in the medical system does not prevent Trusting the Process from feeling confident about vaccinating their children. This suggests that while institutional trust shapes how messages are received, rebuilding trust in the system itself is not a prerequisite for increasing vaccine confidence.

Communication that speaks to each audience's needs and values can drive confidence even in the presence of skepticism.

Doctors Are Trusted, But Transparency Matters

Across all audiences, doctors and nurses are widely viewed as reliable health sources, and most people feel listened to in medical settings. But trust in expertise does not automatically mean people feel fully informed.

Many in Checking the Details and Calling My Own Shots, as well as some in other groups, believe doctors often do not give parents all of the information they need to make good health decisions for their children.

The real dividing line is not trust in doctors' competence but trust in their transparency.

Effective Content Doesn't Look Like Public Health Messaging

By testing found media about vaccines, we found that narrative structure mattered more than content format.

Media that produced statistically reliable positive movement consistently shared:

  • A clear narrative arc
  • Humanized stakes and consequences
  • Grounded vaccination in the lived experiences of families, children, and real-world outcomes

By contrast, content that relied on instruction or the conventions of traditional public health messaging did not produce positive movement.

Humor Must Be Approached With Caution

Humor functions best as a supporting tonal choice, not as the point of the message. In content testing, jokes about vaccines, illness, or pandemics often triggered backlash across audiences.

By contrast, a light-hearted, approachable tone—used to clarify information rather than mock concerns—helped make content feel accessible and credible, especially when humor softened delivery without minimizing stakes.

Vaccine Content Generates Emotional Friction

Across audiences, all vaccine-related content elicited negative emotional responses and reduced positive feelings, regardless of tone or format. This pattern persisted even among content that produced statistically significant positive movement on attitudes.

This suggests that emotional discomfort is a baseline condition of vaccine communication. Effective content does not eliminate negative emotion; it moves audiences through it.

All Stories That Worked Were About Childhood Vaccines

Good news: Videos that worked focused on childhood vaccines. Content about COVID or pandemic-related vaccines did not produce the same positive movement.

This suggests that childhood vaccination holds distinct narrative potential separate from COVID-19 messaging, which may still carry political or pandemic-related baggage.

Audience Profiles: Five Values-Based Audiences

Vaccine confidence in the U.S. is organized into five values-based audiences, each shaped by distinct beliefs, media diets, and narratives. Only one audience lives fully inside the confidence bubble - Trusting the Process. The others occupy spaces of uncertainty or resistance.

Trusting the Process

"Show it's working, and I'm with you."

Core Value: Community

Confidence Driver: System competence, evidence, equity

Caring for My People

"If you say so and I might trust you."

Core Value: Autonomy

Confidence Driver: Practical benefits, fairness, community

Protecting My Family

"Guide me with respect, and I'll protect my kids with you."

Core Value: Order

Confidence Driver: Family protection, responsibility, faith-grounded guidance

Checking the Details

"Help me understand the details, and I can get on board."

Core Value: Achievement

Confidence Driver: Transparent info, concrete numbers, process clarity

Calling My Own Shots

"Respect my judgment, and I'll consider what you say."

Core Value: Authority

Confidence Driver: Parental authority, self-direction, personal responsibility

Where Opportunity Lies

Primary Opportunity

Caring for My People and Checking the Details offer the greatest potential for increasing vaccine confidence. These audiences are motivated by care and responsibility but need alignment, clarity, and reassurance rather than persuasion.

Secondary Opportunity

Protecting My Family represents a secondary opportunity, where confidence can grow when information is calm, values-aligned, and practical.

Guardrail

Calling My Own Shots functions as a guardrail: engagement here should focus on reducing backlash and keeping channels open, not on immediate persuasion.

Amplifier

Trusting the Process serves as an amplifier—reinforcing confidence and enabling positive spillover, but not driving the strategy itself.

Recommendations

1. Tell Complete Stories, Not Messages

Content that produced positive movement consistently followed a clear narrative arc—with a beginning, middle, and end. Stories that showed a situation unfolding, a decision being navigated, and a concrete outcome moved audiences to bigger alignment with the narrative goal.

By contrast, content that resembled public health messaging didn't create greater confidence.

2. Center Real People and Real Stakes

Stories worked best when they were anchored in human experience rather than abstract claims. Content that showed families, caregivers, or clinicians navigating real-world situations, especially around children's health, had more positive impacts.

Stakes were most persuasive when they were practical and relational, not theoretical.

3. Show Vaccination Inside Everyday Life

Content was more engaging when vaccination appeared incidentally within familiar life moments, such as school preparation, routine checkups, travel planning, or caregiving, rather than being framed as a standalone appeal.

Placing vaccination inside everyday contexts may help audiences interpret it as a practical, routine decision, reducing its association with institutional pressure or political debate.

4. Model Responsibility, Rather Than Preach Responsibility

Stories performed best when they showed people acting responsibly instead of being told what responsibility looks like. By depicting thoughtful, protective choices in motion, these pieces reinforced social norms without lecturing or moralizing.

Responsibility became legible through behavior, allowing audiences to see it as achievable and self-directed rather than imposed.

5. Use Humor in Service of the Story, Not as the Story

Humor should be used carefully and in service of the story, not as the story itself. While humor can lower defenses when it supports clarity and respect, it undermines trust when it trivializes illness, heightens anxiety, or displaces children from the center of the narrative.

In emotionally charged contexts, tone should reinforce seriousness of purpose even when moments of levity are present.

6. Design Stories With a Clear Narrative Job

Stories should be designed with a clear narrative job in mind, aligned to a specific dimension of vaccine confidence. The findings show that child development, social norms, and collective benefit do not move together.

Attempting to advance all of these at once risks diluting impact or activating resistance. Greater effectiveness comes from clarity about which belief a story is intended to influence.
